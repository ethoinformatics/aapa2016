But what's in our way?

Our data are really really complex

On a typical day, we might be concerned with:

  • activities and social interactions of animals
  • locations, identities, and availability patterns of key resources
  • meteorological or climatological patterns of occupied sites
  • size, composition, and spatial arrangement of social aggregations
  • life histories of study animals
  • morphological measurements of study animals
  • molecular information derived from biomaterials

Technical obstacles

  • Research data are geographically disjunct
  • Datasets are organized and managed using different practices
    • terminology
    • relationships/schemas
    • software platforms
    • quality control
  • Metadata are seldom documented
  • A community generally trained poorly in information science

Cultural obstacles

  • Concerns about data ownership
    • "A lot of work went into this; I won't just give it to you"
    • "I can't share the data because they don't belong only to me"
  • Concerns about usage of data
    • "You don't know the data like I do"
    • "Why should I expend effort just so you can scoop me?"

"The system will be taken over by research parasites"

Goal of Ethoinformatics

To build capacity for data discovery, data reuse, and data comparison for behavioral research

(with particular emphasis on field primatology)

Project goals

  1. Establish a community-derived standard vocabulary
  2. Create compatible database tools
  3. Create compatible data collection tools
  4. Create tools for translating existing databases
  5. Formulate a framework for long-term data archiving

Summary of working group meetings findings

Databases: points of difference

We see a lot of variation in data management and archiving practices

Some of the variation is related to differences in foci, scale, and longevity of research projects

Databases: points of commonality

Convergence in the ideas of observer, individual, and group as central data collection concepts

Common data structures often emerge from analogous practices

Overall, however, behavioral information are structured inconsistently and these differences are a source of shared frustration

Tools shape our mental models

Our thinking about data is highly influenced by a few dominant tools with outsized influence

Our thinking about data is shaped in tables

It is extremely difficult for many to think about the data model independently of the database

Access

We are vigilant in maintaining and preserving our data, but not about documenting and providing metadata

Ownership, sharing, and privacy are common sources of concern, but there are a range of perspectives within our community

A vast majority of participants reported knowledge of or interest in learning:

  • relational databases (e.g., MS Access, MySQL, PostgreSQL)
  • statistical computing (e.g., R, Python, Julia)

There is a less prevalent interest in learning Web technologies

This is creating a disconnect with rapidly maturing technologies and communities that are increasingly integrated into the Web

What is the Semantic Web?

  • Extension of the World Wide Web
  • Data expressed in RDF (resource description framework)
  • Entities and concepts identified by URI (uniform resource identifiers)

The Semantic Web envisions that webpages serve dual roles in publishing documents for humans and serving data for machines

The links between data will revolutionize web experience just as links between documents did in the early days of the World Wide Web

Linked data refers to structured data shared on the Web to be read by machines

It uses the same technologies and principles as the Semantic Web, but focuses on linking datasets

The relational database

  • Data conform to strict types and constraints
  • Data can be queried, most often using structured query language (SQL)
  • Indexing and querying follow relational logic
  • Records can be joined together using queries

  • Fix bad data
  • Reshape your data
  • Document and annotate your data
  • Write your code for you

Junior vs. senior scientists

  • Junior scientists are more code-savvy
  • Junior scientists have more to gain from "big data"
  • Junior scientists have the incentive to use and extend technology
  • Senior scientists have unique knowledge of their data
  • Senior scientists are gatekeepers for some the largest and most impactful datasets

Two-pronged long-term strategy

  1. Foster an ecosystem of users and technologies that embrace linked data
    • Invest in building tools that people actually want to use
  2. Actively work with curators of major long-term datasets to make their data compatible
    • Analyze and publish collaboratively

Incentivize others to follow our footsteps

Ultimately, annotating and reshaping data will require substantial knowledge and investment from project or database managers

Coding is crucial, and some may need to find coding assistance

Closing thoughts

Data published for human consumption limits machine function, which hinders discovery and reuse

Diversity of data management tools and practices is a strength, not a problem

Researchers should be empowered to use technologies and data structures that are suited for their purposes

Undocumented data = lost data

Metadata are important
(your librarians think about this a lot more than you do)

Technology becomes obsolete

The process is forever (documentation is critical)

We can mitigate obsolescence by committing to open-source technologies with strong user and developer communities

Creating linked capabilities does not itself address data access issues

The broader community ultimately must resolve sharing and licensing concerns

But when the benefits materialize, the culture will change
